This week we learned how to complete the square and how to graph 4 shapes - parabolas, circles, ellipses, and hyperbolas.

**Parabola (quadratic form)**

- Of the form…
  $$y=ax^2+bx+c$$
  $$y=a(x+b)^2+c$$


- The meaning of the completing the square form: $$y=a(x-h)^2+k$$
  - Point $$x=h$$ is the minimum $$x$$ value, since $$x-h$$ where $$x=h$$ is equal to $$0$$. This is the minimum, because we are squaring $$(x-h)$$. Hence why this form is useful.
  - Point $$y=k$$ is the minimum $$y$$ value, since when $$(x-h)^2=0$$ we have the smallest $$y$$ value, which must be $$k$$.


- Turning point of $$(x=-\frac{b}{2a},\ c)$$ or use calculus.


- Types of parabolas
![$$x=-y^2-1$$ (left) and $$x=y^2+1$$ (right)](https://d2mxuefqeaa7sj.cloudfront.net/s_131045A76D752BAE6D78B0A0885E95A97FC9EEA5292F3E15E1DE081A431EAEB6_1551966024443_file.png)

  - Those that go up and down
    - $$y=-x^2+1$$ goes down → note the $$(-)$$ term in front of the $$x^2$$.
    - $$y=x^2+1$$ goes up → note the $$(+)$$ term in front of the $$x^2$$.
  - Sideways parabolas
    - $$x=(y-1)^2$$ or $$x=-y^2-1$$ or $$x = y^2+1$$
    - Turning point of $$(c,\ y=-\frac{b}{2a})$$ or use calculus to determine.

**Circle**

![$$x^2+10x+y^2-4y+20=0$$](https://d2mxuefqeaa7sj.cloudfront.net/s_131045A76D752BAE6D78B0A0885E95A97FC9EEA5292F3E15E1DE081A431EAEB6_1552007669352_file.png)

- Of the form…
  $$x^2+y^2=r^2$$ if center is $$(0,\ 0)$$
  $$(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2$$ if center is $$(h,\ k)$$


- Example $$x^2+10x + y^2-4y+20=0$$:
  - Complete square: $$(x+5)^2+(y-2)^2=9$$
  - Becomes $$(x+5)^2+(y-2)^2=3^2$$
  - Thus, we have center $$(-5,\ 2)$$ and radius $$3$$.


- Other notes:
  - If we have $$a(x-h)^2+b(y-k)^2=r^2$$
    - $$b$$ stretches the circle upwards - smaller $$b$$ means more stretch
    - $$a$$ stretches the circle sideways - smaller $$a$$ means more stretch

**Ellipse**

- Of the form…
![$$x^2-x+9y^2=0$$](https://d2mxuefqeaa7sj.cloudfront.net/s_131045A76D752BAE6D78B0A0885E95A97FC9EEA5292F3E15E1DE081A431EAEB6_1552009375127_file.png)

  $$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2}=1$$ if center is $$(0,\ 0)$$
  - Max/min $$x$$ values at $$\pm{a}$$
  - Max/min $$y$$ values at $$\pm{b}$$
  $$\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}+\frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1$$ if center is $$(h,\ k)$$
  - Max/min $$x$$ values at $$h\pm{a}$$
  - Max/min $$y$$ values at $$k\pm{b}$$


- Example $$x^2-x+9y^2=0$$
  - Complete the square:
    $$(x-\frac{1}{2})^2+9y^2=\frac{1}{4}$$
  - Becomes $$\frac{(x-\frac{1}{2})^2}{(\frac{1}{2})^2}+\frac{y^2}{(1/6)^2} = 1$$
  - Thus, we have center $$(\frac{1}{2},\ 0)$$, max/min $$x$$ values at $$\pm{\frac{1}{2}}$$, and max/min $$y$$ values at $$\pm{\frac{1}{6}}$$. Note that these max/min values are measured from the origin.

**Hyperbola**

- Of the form…
  $$\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}-\frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1$$ if asymptotes intersect at $$(h,\ k)$$ and grows sideways ($$-ve$$ in front of $$y^2$$ term).
  - Cuts $$x$$-axis at $$\pm{a}$$. Does NOT cut $$y$$-axis.
  $$-\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}+\frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1$$ if asymptotes intersect at $$(h,\ k)$$ and grows up and down ($$-ve$$ in front of $$x^2$$ term).
  - Cuts $$y$$-axis at $$\pm{b}$$. Does NOT cut $$x$$-axis.
- Asymptotes take the form…
![$$x^2-4y^2=9$$ in red. Asymptotes in purple and green.](https://d2mxuefqeaa7sj.cloudfront.net/s_131045A76D752BAE6D78B0A0885E95A97FC9EEA5292F3E15E1DE081A431EAEB6_1552011793019_file.png)

  $$y=\pm{\frac{b}{a}x}$$ if centered at the origin
  $$(y-k)=\pm{\frac{b}{a}(x-h)}$$ if centered at $$(h,\ k)$$


- Other notes:
  - As $$x$$ gets large or $$(-)$$large, then the hyperbola tends toward a straight line → the asymptote lines.


- Example: $$x^2-4y^2=9$$
  - Becomes $$\frac{x^2}{3^2}-\frac{y^2}{(\frac{3}{2})^2}=1$$
  - Thus, asymptotes intersect at $$(0,\ 0)$$ and the hyperbola cuts the $$x$$-axis at $$\pm{3}$$.
  

**Ellipse:** $$(term) - (term)$$
**Hyperbola:** $$(term) - (term)$$ **OR** $$-(term)+(term)$$


## Sketching graphs
1. **Standard form**
- Try to rewrite in general form by completing the square.
2. **Classification**
- Parabola (turning point)
- Circle (center, radius)
- Ellipse (center, max/min $$x$$ and $$y$$)
- Hyperbola (center, asymptotes)
3. **Sketch the graph**
- To get $$y$$-intercept, set $$x=0$$.
- To get $$x$$-intercept, set $$y=0$$.


## Completing the square for $$y=x^2+6x+8$$

When we complete the square, we are tying to get back to this form: $$(x+c)^2$$. We are making use of the identity $$x^2+2xh+h^2=(x+h)^2$$.

1. Group the terms for which we want to complete the square: $$y=(x^2+6x)+8$$
2. Now take the coefficient of $$x$$, half it, square it, and add it inside the brackets. Then subtract it outside the brackets: $$y=(x^2+6x + (\frac{6}{2})^2)+8-(\frac{6}{2})^2$$
3. We can now simplify what we have to $$y=(x^2+6x+9)-1$$.
4. What is inside of the brackets can now be simplified to $$(x+3)^2$$. Essentially we are just changing it back to squared form by adding the square root of $$9$$ to $$x$$ and squaring it. We now have a factorized version of the original equation: $$(x+3)^2-1$$.


- For this quadratic, the point $$(-3,\ -1)$$, is the turning point. Minimum in this case.
